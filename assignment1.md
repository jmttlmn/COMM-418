## [Internal ICE records reveal widespread use of force in detention centers](https://www.washingtonpost.com/business/2026/05/04/ice-detention-centers-force/)
### Washington Post

I chose the Washington Post story about use of force in ICE detention centers. My main reaction is that the data made the story much stronger than it would have been with only interviews or a few examples. The reporters used internal ICE reports to show patterns across many detention centers over time. That helped them prove that the issue was not just one bad incident or one bad facility. The numbers showed increases in use-of-force cases and in the number of detainees affected.

The story made it pretty clear where the data came from. It explained that the records were internal ICE reports and that the Post reviewed and organized them. It also said some of the cleaned records were posted publicly, with private information removed. That made the reporting feel more transparent. Still, one unclear part was how complete the data really was. The article admitted that some facilities were missing and some reports were vague, so the real number of incidents could be higher.

What I liked most was that the story connected the data to real people. The numbers showed scale, but the interviews made the issue easier to understand. The story did not treat detainees as just statistics. It showed what the data meant in actual detention conditions.

The data was presented mostly through words, summary numbers and some visual/video elements. That worked, but I think the story could have been improved with a simple map or searchable table showing which facilities had the most incidents. That would make it easier for readers to compare places.

I am still wondering which private contractors had the worst records and whether ICE or DHS punished anyone after these incidents. From here, I would investigate specific facilities, compare contractors and look at whether complaints led to real consequences.
