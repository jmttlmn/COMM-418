# The Camera That Has Saved Lives

The intersection of Alabama Ave SE and Stanton Road had been a common spot for collisions in D.C.'s Eighth Ward. However, data suggests that since the city installed a red light camera at the intersection in late 2024, crashes have plummeted.

Between 2019 and 2024, the intersection averaged about 33 crashes a year or a little under three a month. Last year, the first full year with the camera, only 16 crashes were reported, with 2026 on pace for eight, the lowest number of annual crashes at the intersection since data became reliable. 

The camera, which has consistently been a top 10 ticket-giver compared to other red light cameras in D.C., according to DDOT's website, appears to be doing exactly what traffic enforcement officials hope these devices will do: change driver behavior. A camera that issued 568 tickets last month, each costing $150, might seem controversial at first, but at this intersection, the results point to a clear public safety effect. 

While one intersection does not settle the larger debate over automated traffic enforcement, especially speed cameras, which have little evidence to suggest they prevent crashes, the numbers at this intersection are difficult to ignore. A location that once averaged more than 30 crashes a year has seen that total cut roughly in half after the camera's installation, and current trends suggest the number may fall even further. At least at Alabama Avenue SE and Stanton Road, the red light camera seems to be working.

### Details 
Cleaning the data was a bit of a process. I needed to first clear a lot of rows and figure out how to determine if a crash occurred at that specific intersection. My first idea was to use addresses and a filter to only include crashes which occured at addresses near the intersection. This proved to be inconsistent as addresses were labeled in lots of different ways. Next, I thought about using a bounding box as the coordinates of each crash were listed in my dataset. However, again, this was inconsistent as these coordinates were not exact and resulted in crashes being included that shouldn't have been, or vice versa. Finally, I discovered the Route ID column, and this was a game-changer. Each road in D.C. has a Route ID, and by filtering to only include crashes which occured on either Alabama Ave SE or Stanton Road, while also filtering for the closest intersection, gave me the most accurate results. After filtering for direction of travel, I was given my dataset of crashes which occured at that intersection. I should also note that I found, based purely on the annual crashes, that the larger data set sees an impossible decline in annual crashes after 2015. This led me to believe that the data was incomplete before this (or they changed how they input it). I am not certain on this, but it is the only explanation I have for the drastic drop-off in annual crashes citywide.
